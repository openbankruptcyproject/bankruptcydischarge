Discharge Revoked -- Section 727(d)

In rare cases, a discharge can be revoked after entry. The requirements are strict, the deadlines are short, and it is extremely uncommon.

Grounds for Revocation

Under Section 727(d), a discharge may be revoked if:

  1. 727(d)(1) -- Fraud: The discharge was obtained through fraud of the debtor, and the requesting party did not know about the fraud until after the discharge was granted
  2. 727(d)(2) -- Concealment: The debtor acquired, or became entitled to, property of the estate and knowingly and fraudulently failed to report or deliver it to the trustee
  3. 727(d)(3) -- Refusal to obey: The debtor refused to obey any lawful order of the court or refused to testify (after being granted immunity)

The standard is fraud. Honest mistakes or negligent omissions are not grounds for revocation. The requesting party must prove that the debtor acted with fraudulent intent.

The One-Year Deadline

The request for revocation must be filed within one year after the discharge was granted or, for ground (2) above, before the case is closed (whichever is later).

After the one-year deadline, the discharge is permanent and cannot be challenged. This provides finality for debtors and confidence that the fresh start is secure.

How Rare Is Revocation?

Discharge revocation is extremely uncommon. The vast majority of discharges are never challenged after entry. Revocation proceedings require:

  • Clear evidence of fraud or concealment
  • Filing within the strict deadline
  • A full hearing with opportunity for the debtor to respond

Most trustees and creditors who discover issues will raise them before the discharge enters (as objections under 727(a)) rather than seeking revocation after the fact.

Chapter 13 Revocation -- Section 1328(e)

Chapter 13 discharges can be revoked under Section 1328(e) if:

  • The discharge was obtained through fraud
  • The request is filed within one year of the discharge
  • The requesting party did not know about the fraud before the discharge

Like Chapter 7 revocation, this is extremely rare in practice. The debtor has already completed 3-5 years of plan payments, and fraud discovered that late is unusual.
